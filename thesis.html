MSc Thesis – Directed Energy Counter-UAV Systems

Executive Summary

The global proliferation of low-cost UAVs has introduced significant security and privacy risks, particularly in urban and conflict environments. Traditional counter-UAV solutions such as kinetic interception or RF jamming are often costly, legally constrained, or unsuitable for populated areas.

While modern high-energy laser systems neutralize drones through structural destruction, they require kilowatt-class power levels and complex hardware. This thesis proposes an alternative concept: suppressing UAV optical sensors using lower-power, high-precision lasers.

The approach shifts complexity from laser power generation to tracking and control accuracy. A visually tracked UAV trajectory feeds a two-axis gimbal-mounted laser system, where alignment precision determines the achieved Time on Target (TOT). Through dynamic modelling, control design, and simulation, the feasibility of this concept is evaluated across realistic operational scenarios.

Structural overview and summary

1. Introduction

1.1 Motivation

The increasing accessibility of drone technology has created new security vulnerabilities worldwide, reinforced by their prominent use in modern conflicts. Urban and law enforcement scenarios demand countermeasures that minimize collateral damage while remaining economically sustainable.

1.2 Related Work

Existing research spans three primary subsystems:

  • Gimbal Dynamics: Modelled using Newton–Euler, Lagrange, or Kane formulations, each offering different advantages in handling nonlinearities and coupling effects.
  • Visual Tracking & State Estimation: YOLO-based detection combined with monocular scaling for range estimation, refined using Extended Kalman Filtering to mitigate pixel jitter and localization noise.
  • Laser Systems: High-energy laser applications typically focus on structural destruction (10–100 kW). This project instead investigates lower-power operation aimed at sensor dazzling or permanent optical damage.

1.3 Problem Overview

A UAV follows a predefined or evasive trajectory and is detected via a monocular vision system. Estimated position and velocity measurements drive a 2-DOF gimbal-mounted laser platform. Laser effectiveness depends on delivered irradiance, atmospheric attenuation, beam divergence, and incidence angle.

System success is quantified through Time on Target (TOT), representing the duration of sufficient alignment and energy transfer to the UAV’s optical sensor.

1.4 Research Objectives

  • Develop and compare three nonlinear gimbal dynamic models.
  • Integrate control strategies and sweeping protocols.
  • Incorporate realistic tracking noise and atmospheric laser effects.
  • Evaluate feasibility across varying laser power levels and engagement conditions.
  • Generate interception envelopes for nominal and evasive scenarios.

1.5 Thesis Structure

  • Chapter 2: Theoretical modelling and control framework.
  • Chapter 3: Simulation architecture and proposed work.
  • Chapter 4: Evaluation and conclusions.
